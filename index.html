📖 Research Rationale

Isabela de Basilan sits at the intersection of four realities: deep indigenous ecological knowledge, a highly specialised fisheries economy (LQ 3.84), full diesel dependency, and two CADTs still pending for three ICCs. Economic analysis that observes these conditions without being co-built with the communities living them cannot produce durable, legitimate interventions.

Dr. Sixto K. Roxas III put it sharply: "Economists should build economies. Not observe them — build them." That is the stance of this research. Community OS treats Isabela as Pilot Community #1 of 250 — not as a data point, but as a co-designing partner whose knowledge is a primary source, not a supplementary one.

The rationale is threefold

  1. Empirical — existing PSA 240-sector data, BFAR, BASELCO/BAPCO, and LGU BDIP records give a credible baseline, but none of them describe the informal economy (T9) or traditional ecological knowledge (T7). CEESAM 240×240 validated with community input closes that gap.
  2. Methodological — PEAR plus ECSOM plus Sikolohiyang Pilipino plus Community Psychology is, to our knowledge, a novel four-root synthesis in Philippine participatory research. Isabela is where that synthesis is stress-tested.
  3. Ethical & political — FPIC, IPRA, and co-authorship rights are not compliance checkboxes. They are the operating conditions under which this research is legitimate at all.

🔄 Theory of Change

IF the indigenous communities of Isabela de Basilan — Yakan, Sama Banguingui, and Sama Dilaut — together with the wider population mix (which includes Tausug and settler residents) co-design and validate a CEESAM Input-Output matrix, co-author the ECSOM Canvas Narrative, and co-own the intervention prototypes that follow,

THEN the resulting evidence base will reveal Win-Win-Win investment priorities (Solar RE, Seaweed SRC, Cold Chain + Halal Cluster, CADT Fast-Track, Digital Economy) that are simultaneously ecologically sustainable, economically productive, and culturally grounded,

BECAUSE community validation produces interventions that withstand political cycles, attract aligned capital (see UNDP Aligning Capital framework), and are legible to both institutional and indigenous governance systems,

SO THAT the Community OS model can be replicated across 250 pilot communities nationally and translated into BIMP-EAGA regional integration frameworks — with co-authorship as a right, not a bonus.

❓ Research Questions

How can a participatory, four-root research architecture produce an evidence base that communities themselves recognise as their own — and that institutional actors treat as authoritative?

This breaks into five operational research questions:

  1. How does the Isabela de Basilan economy actually function across its 240 sectors when informal, household, and indigenous-knowledge activities are made visible alongside the formal economy?
  2. Which Win-Win-Win intervention priorities do the communities recognise as legitimate — and how do their priorities align or diverge from the desktop-derived shortlist?
  3. What governance and ethical conditions (FPIC, CADT, co-authorship protocols) need to be institutionalised for community validation to be durable beyond a single project cycle?
  4. How does co-designing intervention prototypes in Phase 2 change the structure and confidence of the findings established in Phase 1?
  5. What elements of the Community OS toolkit transfer to the other 249 pilot communities — and what has to be re-invented locally in each?

🧪 Research Overview — The 21-Week Community OS Installation

The programme is structured as a 21-week installation of the Community OS in Isabela de Basilan — not as a conventional "research project" that begins and ends. It is the setup of a local research-and-action capacity that the community will operate itself.

PHASE 1 · WEEKS 1–5
Research & Validation
Evidence, co-validation, and return to community

The research arc: desktop triangulation, administrative KIIs, FPIC documentation, community KIIs and FGDs, photovoice sessions, and co-verification of the CEESAM 240×240 matrix. Findings are brought back to the community before any institutional audience sees them.

  • Week 1 — Ethical review gate, FPIC, admin kit
  • Weeks 2–3 — CEESAM validation begins
  • Weeks 4–5 — KIIs, FGDs, photovoice, community return
PHASE 2 · WEEKS 6–21
Installation & Co-Design
Knowledge mobilisation & community-determined next steps

The installation arc: the evidence becomes action. Weeks 6–11 run CMC critical-components assessment and early co-design. From Week 12 onward, the specific activities are shaped by the Community Management Council (CMC).

  • Weeks 6–8 — Community research exhibit + town hall
  • Weeks 9–11 — CMC critical-components assessment
  • Weeks 12–21 — CMC-determined: co-design, intervention design, policy briefs, final exhibit, CMC launch
⚠ CMC-Determined Window Weeks 12–21 will be determined by the community through the Community Management Council (CMC). The specific sequencing, depth, and focus of co-design workshops, intervention design, policy briefs, and the final exhibit are set by the community — not pre-scripted by the research team.

Phase 2 activities (CMC will sequence and prioritise these)

  • Community research exhibits and town hall
  • CMC critical-components assessment — what governance infrastructure needs to hold?
  • Co-designing intervention prototypes — workshops with each community, then jointly
  • Intervention design workshops — refinement, costing, alignment with UNDP Aligning Capital
  • Policy briefs — for LGU, BARMM, and BIMP-EAGA audiences
  • Final exhibit and CMC launch — handover to the community-led governance body

The gate between the two phases is the Week 5 community return and Week 6 town hall. That is where the community decides whether and how the research continues into installation.

Formal Enterprise
Registered businesses with formal employment (seaweed processors, boat builders).
T2
Informal Livelihood
Unregistered micro-enterprises and cottage industries.
T3
Household Economy
Subsistence production for household use and local barter.
T4
Cooperative Member
Participants in formalised groups with collective marketing.
T5
Youth Entrepreneur
Young people (18–35) with growing businesses and tech adoption.

Women Leader

Indigenous Knowledge Holder
