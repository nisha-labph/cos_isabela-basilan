📖 Research Rationale

Isabela de Basilan sits at the intersection of four realities: deep indigenous ecological knowledge, a highly specialised fisheries economy (LQ 3.84), full diesel dependency, and two CADTs still pending for three ICCs. Economic analysis that observes these conditions without being co-built with the communities living them cannot produce durable, legitimate interventions.

Dr. Sixto K. Roxas III put it sharply: "Economists should build economies. Not observe them — build them." That is the stance of this research. Community OS treats Isabela as Pilot Community #1 of 250 — not as a data point, but as a co-designing partner whose knowledge is a primary source, not a supplementary one.

The rationale is therefore threefold:

  1. Empirical — existing PSA 240-sector data, BFAR, BASELCO/BAPCO, and LGU BDIP records give a credible baseline, but none of them describe the informal economy (T9) or traditional ecological knowledge (T7). CEESAM 240×240 validated with community input closes that gap.
  2. Methodological — PEAR plus ECSOM plus Sikolohiyang Pilipino plus Community Psychology is, to our knowledge, a novel four-root synthesis in Philippine participatory research. Isabela is where that synthesis is stress-tested.
  3. Ethical & political — FPIC, IPRA, and co-authorship rights are not compliance checkboxes. They are the operating conditions under which this research is legitimate at all.

🔄 Theory of Change

IF the four indigenous nations of Isabela de Basilan co-design and validate a CEESAM Input-Output matrix, co-author the ECSOM Canvas Narrative, and co-own the intervention prototypes that follow,

THEN the resulting evidence base will reveal Win-Win-Win investment priorities (Solar RE, Seaweed SRC, Cold Chain + Halal Cluster, CADT Fast-Track, Digital Economy) that are simultaneously ecologically sustainable, economically productive, and culturally grounded,

BECAUSE community validation produces interventions that withstand political cycles, attract aligned capital (see UNDP Aligning Capital framework), and are legible to both institutional and indigenous governance systems,

SO THAT the Community OS model can be replicated across 250 pilot communities nationally and translated into BIMP-EAGA regional integration frameworks — with co-authorship as a right, not a bonus.

❓ Research Questions

The overarching question is: How can a participatory, four-root research architecture produce an evidence base that communities themselves recognise as their own — and that institutional actors treat as authoritative?

This breaks down into five operational research questions:

  1. How does the Isabela de Basilan economy actually function across its 240 sectors when informal, household, and indigenous-knowledge activities are made visible alongside the formal economy?
  2. Which Win-Win-Win intervention priorities do the four indigenous nations recognise as legitimate — and how do their priorities align or diverge from the desktop-derived shortlist?
  3. What governance and ethical conditions (FPIC, CADT, co-authorship protocols) need to be institutionalised for community validation to be durable beyond a single project cycle?
  4. How does co-designing intervention prototypes in Weeks 12–20 change the structure and confidence of the CEESAM findings established in Weeks 2–11?
  5. What elements of the Community OS toolkit transfer to the other 249 pilot communities — and what has to be re-invented locally in each?

🧪 Research Overview

The programme runs across 20 weeks, organised around two major arcs:

Weeks 1–11 · Evidence & Validation. CEESAM validation ideally begins in Week 2 or Week 3 and is continuous through Week 11. This window includes desktop triangulation, administrative KIIs, FPIC documentation, community KIIs, FGDs, photovoice sessions, and co-verification of the CEESAM matrix. By the end of Week 11, the analysis and the preliminary report must be ready for presentation.

Weeks 12–20 · Knowledge Mobilisation & Co-Design. Beginning with community research exhibits and a town hall, the second arc is where the evidence becomes action:

  • Community research exhibits + town hall — findings returned to the community first, before any institutional audience
  • CMC (Community Management Council) critical-components assessment — what governance infrastructure needs to be in place for interventions to hold?
  • Co-designing intervention prototypes — workshops with each of the four nations separately, then jointly
  • Intervention design workshops — refinement, costing, and alignment with UNDP Aligning Capital framework
  • Policy briefs — for LGU, BARMM, and BIMP-EAGA audiences
  • Final exhibit and CMC launch — handover to the community-led governance body

The gate between the two arcs — the Week 11 presentation — is where the community decides whether and how the research continues into Phase 2.

💡 ProSumer Typologies (T1–T9)

Community participant profiles used for stratified sampling and narrative analysis.

T1
Formal Enterprise
Registered businesses with formal employment (seaweed processors, boat builders).
T2
Informal Livelihood
Unregistered micro-enterprises and cottage industries.
T3
Household Economy
Subsistence production for household use and local barter.
T4
Cooperative Member
Participants in formalised groups with collective marketing.
T5
Youth Entrepreneur
Young people (18–35) with growing businesses and tech adoption.
T6
Women Leader
Women in household decision-making or business ownership.
T7
Indigenous Knowledge Holder
Community members with traditional ecological knowledge.
T8
Government/NGO Partner
LGU officials and institutional stakeholders.
T9
Informal Economy Household
Non-working household members as consumers in the local economy.
