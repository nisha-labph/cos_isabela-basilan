Research Approach

PEAR Methodology Explained

Participatory Emancipatory Action Research holds that knowledge production and social transformation are inseparable. Research here is not extractive — it does not take data from a community and return findings to an academic audience elsewhere. The community is the co-producer of knowledge, the co-designer of change, and the co-owner of all outputs.

The communities most affected by structural inequity are the best positioned to understand and transform it — if given the tools, language, and legitimacy to do so.

This programme applies Haklay's (2013) taxonomy of participation at its highest level — "extreme citizen science" — where community members define the research agenda, collect and interpret data, and co-author all outputs. Every research instrument was co-designed; every finding goes back to the community for validation before it goes anywhere else.

1
PEAR / Citizen Social Science
Jallad et al. (2021) · UCL RELIEF Centre

Participatory Emancipatory Action Research positions the community not as an object of study but as an active co-producer of knowledge. The Hamra Prosperity Model — developed in Lebanon — provides the methodological template: communities define prosperity on their own terms, identify their own structural barriers, and co-design their own interventions. This programme applies that logic to Isabela de Basilan, grounded in the citizen social science tradition that recognises local knowledge as legitimate and rigorous evidence.

Read the foundational paper
2
ECSOM / CEESAM
Dr. Sixto K. Roxas III · FCOMT 1984 → Community OS 2026

Where conventional economics asks "How profitable is the enterprise?", ECSOM asks "How well is the community sustaining itself — ecologically, economically, socially, and culturally — across generations?" CEESAM is the quantitative instrument that makes this operational: a structured accounting matrix that captures all major flows and stocks in a community's life — economic production, ecological footprints, social equity, and cultural heritage — in one integrated system. Three-column canvas: Ecosystem · Community · Sustainability.

Read the ECSOM/CEESAM explainer
3
Sikolohiyang Pilipino
Enriquez (1975) · Indigenous Filipino Psychology

The research is grounded in the ethical and relational framework of indigenous Filipino psychology. The concept of kapwa — shared identity, the recognition of the self in the other — orients all researcher-community relationships. Knowledge flows from within the culture, not toward it. All sessions are conducted in Filipino and the local vernacular. Talakayan — structured communal dialogue aimed at collective discernment — is the primary workshop format throughout the programme.

Read the Sikolohiyang Pilipino explainer
4
Community Psychology
Bronfenbrenner · SAMHSA · Deci & Ryan SDT

Community Psychology is the science of understanding how social, economic, and environmental conditions shape wellbeing — and how communities can work together to change those conditions. It brings together research and action, community members and experts, local knowledge and evidence, to build communities that are more just, more resilient, and more capable of shaping their own futures. Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems model frames the nested contexts of Isabela de Basilan's communities. SAMHSA trauma-informed principles apply given BARMM's post-conflict history.

Kapwa
Shared identity — the recognition of the self in the other. The relational and ethical foundation of all researcher-community interactions. Researchers are not neutral outsiders but co-members of the community of inquiry.
Community Dialogue
Talakayan
Structured communal dialogue aimed at collective discernment. The primary workshop format throughout the 21-week programme — replacing lecture or presentation with shared sensemaking.
ECSOM Investment Logic
Win-Win-Win
Every proposed intervention must simultaneously benefit the community (people), the ecosystem (planet), and the local economy (sustainability). No single-axis optimisation — all three columns of the ECSOM canvas must show positive movement.
CEESAM Typology
ProSumer
Producer-Consumer — the CEESAM's core unit of analysis. Every household and group is simultaneously a producer of goods, services, or ecological value and a consumer of resources. Eight ProSumer typologies are mapped across Isabela de Basilan.
Freirean Foundation
Conscientização
Freire's critical consciousness — the process by which communities understand the structural forces shaping their lives and develop the agency to transform them. Embedded in Paaralang Kapuluan orientation workshops.

Research Foundations

Rationale, Theory of Change & Research Questions

Research Rationale

Isabela de Basilan presents a concentrated case study in unrealised endogenous potential: substantial ecological and cultural capital, demonstrated comparative advantage in seaweed and marine sectors, yet persistent poverty sustained by trade structures that channel value away from local communities before it can be retained and reinvested.

Conventional development approaches have treated communities as recipients of programmes designed elsewhere. The PEAR-ECSOM approach inverts this: communities become the architects of their own economic systems, building the internal capacities — analytical, institutional, and productive — needed to direct their own development trajectories. The CEESAM IO Matrix serves as the quantitative instrument that shows communities precisely where value circulates, where it dissipates, and where local retention is structurally achievable.

The Isabela pilot tests whether this integrated model — combining rigorous quantitative accounting with participatory emancipatory methodology — can strengthen community economic agency in ways that are replicable across BARMM and beyond. The framework is designed to be transferable to other municipalities and island economies with comparable structural profiles, wherever communities seek to build the internal capacity to manage their own ecological and economic systems.

Core Hypothesis

If communities are equipped with accurate data about their own economic system — and participate fully in designing interventions — they will generate more durable, equitable, and ecologically viable development outcomes than externally imposed programmes.

The CEESAM validation process tests whether secondary-data estimates of production flows, ecological deficits, and livelihood multipliers hold up under community scrutiny — and whether community-generated primary data changes the picture in ways that alter intervention priorities.

Research Questions

RQ1

What does prosperity mean to residents of Isabela de Basilan — across gender, age, ethnic community, and sector — and how does that definition compare to the ECSOM secondary-data baseline?

Cluster A — Prosperity Mapping · Weeks 3–5

RQ2

What are the structural root causes of livelihood insecurity and ecological degradation in Isabela de Basilan, as identified by community members through participatory root cause analysis?

Cluster B — Root Cause Analysis · Weeks 6–11

RQ3

How do existing governance structures and institutional arrangements in Isabela de Basilan support or obstruct community-controlled economic development — particularly for indigenous communities?

Cluster C — Governance Mapping · Weeks 12–14

RQ4

Does the CEESAM 240×240 IO Matrix accurately reflect Isabela de Basilan's actual economic flows, and what adjustments does community-generated primary data require?

Continuous CEESAM Validation — Weeks 1–21

RQ5

To what extent is the Community OS methodology — PEAR-ECSOM integrated — replicable across BARMM territories and applicable as a community development model in comparable island economies?
